Welcome to Social Engineering Awareness

This guide is designed to teach you everything you need to know about social engineering attacks. By reading carefully, you will understand how attackers operate, recognize warning signs, and learn the practical steps to protect yourself. Even beginners will come away feeling confident and prepared.

Phishing

Phishing attacks involve a person or group pretending to be a trustworthy source in order to steal sensitive information. They may send emails, messages, or create fake websites that appear authentic. Phishing can happen to anyone and is one of the most common attacks because it targets human trust rather than technical weaknesses.

Example: You receive an email from "Your IT Team" stating your account password will expire today. The email contains a link asking you to reset it immediately.
Tip: Phishing emails create urgency. Pause, analyze the sender, and verify the request through official channels before taking any action.

Why It Works:

  • Exploits emotions: fear, urgency, or curiosity
  • Often looks legitimate with company logos or personal details
  • Most people act quickly without verifying details

Common Warning Signs:

  • Emails from unknown or suspicious senders
  • Links that don't match official websites
  • Requests for passwords, financial data, or personal information
  • Spelling/grammar errors in otherwise professional messages
  • Urgency cues: "Act now" or "Immediate action required"

Steps to Stay Safe:

  • Never click links or attachments from unknown sources
  • Verify requests with official contacts (call or email your IT Team directly)
  • Report suspicious emails immediately

Baiting

Baiting lures people into a trap by offering something appealing, such as free downloads, gifts, or devices. The goal is to trick users into installing malware or compromising security. Curiosity and the desire for rewards are what baiting exploits.

Example: A USB stick labeled "Confidential Reports" is left on your desk. Plugging it into your computer could install malware silently.
Tip: Never plug in unknown devices. Report found devices to your IT Team.

Steps to Stay Safe:

  • Ignore suspicious links, downloads, or gifts
  • Never connect unknown USBs or devices
  • Report suspicious items immediately

Pretexting

Pretexting occurs when an attacker invents a story or identity to gain your trust and obtain sensitive information. They may impersonate a colleague, a service provider, or an authority figure. The goal is to manipulate you into giving information you normally wouldn’t share.

Example: A caller claims to be from "Your Bank" and asks you to verify account details for "security purposes."
Tip: Verify every request through official channels. Do not be pressured by urgency or authority.

Steps to Stay Safe:

  • Never give out passwords or financial information without verification
  • Contact official sources to confirm requests
  • Report suspicious interactions to IT/security

Additional Steps to Protect Yourself

Adopting these daily habits significantly reduces your risk of falling victim to social engineering attacks. Each tip includes an explanation and example so you can apply it in real-life scenarios.

  • Create strong passwords: Use at least 12 characters combining uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols. Example: Instead of "password123," use "T3al!B1rd92#". Strong passwords reduce the risk of brute force attacks.
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA): Adds an extra verification step such as a code sent to your phone. Example: Even if someone guesses your password, they cannot access your account without the code.
  • Keep software updated: Install updates on devices, browsers, and apps to patch security vulnerabilities. Example: Security patches often fix vulnerabilities attackers could exploit.
  • Verify links and attachments: Only open content from trusted sources. Example: Hover over links to check the URL, or confirm with your IT team before opening attachments.
  • Lock devices: Use PINs, passwords, or fingerprints. Example: A locked laptop prevents unauthorized access if left unattended.
  • Backup important files: Keep offline or cloud backups. Example: If malware encrypts your files, you can restore them safely.
  • Use secure networks: Avoid public Wi-Fi for sensitive accounts. Example: Logging into accounts over open Wi-Fi increases the risk of interception.
  • Limit sharing personal information: Only share with trusted sources. Example: Avoid posting full date of birth or ID numbers online.
  • Stay informed: Learn about emerging tactics. Example: Follow security blogs or updates from your IT team to recognize new phishing trends.
  • Report suspicious activity: Notify IT/security immediately. Example: If you receive a suspicious email claiming to be from your IT team, report it rather than ignoring it.
